What research infrastructure for high-stakes space operations requires, and why I think it's one of the more interesting problems in the field right now.

01 · The Core Tension

The people Slingshot builds for
can't wait for insight.

Neither can the research that supports them.

Analysts tracking conjunction events in real time, operators executing maneuver planning under pressure, decision-makers synthesizing multi-source data during high-stakes scenarios. This is a user population that operates with a different cognitive profile than most software users. They have deep domain expertise, low tolerance for friction, and limited margin for tools that aren't working exactly as expected.

That combination creates a research problem that standard UXR playbooks aren't quite designed for. The typical cycle of research, synthesis, handoff, and eventual integration is too slow for the pace at which Slingshot needs to learn. And the risk profile of getting it wrong is different when the downstream consequences of friction include mission-critical decisions.

The question underneath all of it: how do you build research infrastructure that's rigorous enough to produce trustworthy findings and embedded enough to actually move product decisions in a fast-moving environment? That's the problem I find genuinely interesting about this role.

What most UXR functions optimize for
Research-to-handoff cycle time
Insight volume per quarter
Stakeholder satisfaction with deliverables
Coverage of product surface area
Usability benchmarks vs. baseline
What research has to do here
Reduce decision latency for product teams
Build operator mental model fidelity
Surface friction before it hits mission workflows
Make research reusable across product teams
Close the loop on what actually changed
The gap between those two columns is a research ops design problem, not just a methods problem.
02 · What the UXR Page Signals

You've already built something
most orgs at this stage haven't.

That's worth being specific about.

The UXR page at slingshot.space/uxresearch isn't just a recruitment vehicle. It's a research infrastructure artifact. The Learn-Build-Measure loop you've formalized, the distinction between foundational workflow studies and quantitative validation, the participant community with SPU incentives tied to actual platform activity: these are structural choices that reflect a real point of view about how research and product should interact.

Most companies at Slingshot's stage don't build a public-facing research community with formalized participation tiers. The fact that this exists tells me something about how you think about the function: research is something the organization builds with users, not just a discovery layer that precedes design.

What's interesting about running foundational studies alongside the continuous feedback loop you've described is that they require different recruiting pipelines, different analysis workflows, and different relationships with product teams to be effective at the same time. Building that infrastructure while running the research is the hard problem. That's where I'd want to contribute.

04 · What I'd Focus On First

Not a list of deliverables.
A set of structural bets.

Based on what's visible from the outside.

Research Ops
Make the community a research asset, not just a recruiting list

The UXR Community infrastructure exists. The question is whether it's generating longitudinal insight or one-time participation. Designing the taxonomy and tagging system for what you learn from community members is the difference between a panel and a strategic data asset.

Methods
Instrument the conjunction scenario for research, not just telemetry

High-pressure workflow moments are where mental model failures surface. Building research protocols specifically around those moments, diary studies, contextual inquiry in operational environments, surfaces what product analytics can't.

Integration
Close the loop on what changes because of research

The research impact section on your public page names the right outcomes: friction reduction, adoption through trust, workflow continuity. Building the internal tracking that ties specific research findings to those outcomes is what makes the function legible to leadership.

Scale
Build templates that hold up without the senior researcher in the room

Research ops isn't administration. It's the infrastructure that lets a growing team run consistent, high-quality studies without starting from scratch each time. I've built this before in contexts where the research team was small relative to the product surface area.

The intersection of mission-critical operations, a research team that's actively building its infrastructure, and a product space that's genuinely hard to understand from the outside is not a combination that comes up often. I'd like to talk about how you're thinking about the function's next phase.

I've worked in high-stakes, high-expertise user environments my entire career. This feels like a natural extension of that work, not a stretch.
